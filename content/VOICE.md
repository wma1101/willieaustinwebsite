# Willie Austin — Voice Guide

Use this document when writing any content for the site.
Copy and paste it as a system prompt if using an AI writing tool.

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

A smart person talking about fashion at a dinner table — confident, specific, unhurried, and never trying to impress you.

---

## Rhythm & Structure

**The em dash is the signature move.**
Not parenthetical. It's a pivot, a breath, a "but actually" — it redirects the thought mid-sentence. Use it constantly.

> "The self-taught approach shows in his work — not as a limitation, but as a freedom."

**Short sentence after a long one.** Let a thought build, then land it in five words.

> "The scissors know the difference between a careless cut and a careful one, even if the result looks the same. The fabric knows."

**"Not X, but Y" construction.** This is how Willie reframes ideas — he tells you what something isn't first, then what it is.

> "Not as provocation, but as a genuine design language."
> "Not press releases or marketing copy, but genuine essays."
> "Not as a gimmick, but as a fundamental rethinking of what a garment is."

**Open with a declaration, not a question.** Start paragraphs with something definitive.

> "Consider the dart."
> "This is not laziness disguised as design."
> "There is something profoundly honest about a garment that shows its making."

**End with something short and final.** The last line of a section should hit like a door closing.

> "That's the project."
> "I'm just busy."
> "Fashion breathes."
> "It's a vocabulary."

## Word Choices

**Use physical, tactile words.** Willie thinks through his hands. The writing should feel like you can touch it.

YES: scissors, bolt of cloth, selvage, muslin, cutting table, steam, thread, fabric swatches, half-finished, raw edge, seam, hem, dart, draping, pressed flat

NO: creative journey, design thinking, aesthetic vision, innovative approach

**Use architecture and music words for structure.** Fashion and buildings share a vocabulary.

YES: vocabulary, language, conversation, movement, tension, structure, fluidity, silhouette, palette, composition

NO: vibe, energy, moment, iconic, stunning, breathtaking

**Name real designers and references casually.** Don't explain who they are. If the reader doesn't know, that's fine. It signals you belong in the conversation.

> "Rei Kawakubo has been asking it since the early 1980s. Martin Margiela turned it into a philosophy."

Not: "Legendary designer Rei Kawakubo, known for her avant-garde approach..."

---

## Tone Rules

1. **No exclamation marks.** Ever. Excitement is expressed through specificity, not punctuation.

2. **No hedge words.** Don't write "It's worth noting" or "It could be argued" or "In many ways." Just say the thing.

3. **No empty adjectives.** "Innovative," "cutting-edge," "revolutionary," "stunning," "iconic" — these words mean nothing. Replace them with something specific or delete them.

4. **No explaining fashion to outsiders.** Write as if the reader already cares. You don't need to justify why fashion matters. You're not convincing anyone. You're already in the room and so are they.

5. **No corporate language.** "Leverage," "synergy," "elevate the conversation," "push boundaries" — none of it.

6. **Humor is dry and comes from honesty.** It's never a joke. It's just the truth said plainly.

> "I'm not against trends. I'm just busy."
> "Dodging Anna Wintour..."

7. **Confidence without ego.** State opinions as facts. Don't say "I believe" or "In my opinion" — just say it. But never punch down or name-drop to flex.

## What Willie Writes About

Every piece connects back to one of these ideas:

- **Making is thinking.** The physical act of cutting and sewing is how ideas take shape. Process isn't preparation for the work — it is the work.
- **Fashion and editorial are the same thing.** Clothes are text. Writing is design. The separation is artificial.
- **Show the work.** Visible seams, raw edges, exposed construction — honesty as a design choice. This applies to writing too: show the thinking, not just the conclusion.
- **Tension between opposites.** Structure and fluidity. Control and release. Darkness and warmth. The interesting space is always where two things coexist.

## Sentence Examples to Study

These are the lines that define the voice. Read them before writing anything.

"Every collection is a story. The clothes are the text. The editorial is how you read them."

"I kept seeing things in my head that didn't exist yet. At some point, the only option was to make them myself."

"Walk into any fabric store and run your hand along a bolt of cloth. Feel the selvage — that finished edge where the weft threads turn back on themselves."

"The process isn't linear. Ideas fold back on themselves. A sleeve that worked in sketch didn't work in muslin. A color that seemed wrong became the foundation of the whole palette. Design is finding your way by getting lost."

"Every seam is intentional. Every fold is considered. But nothing is rigid. The clothes move because the person wearing them moves. That's the difference between architecture and fashion — fashion breathes."

"The industry has trained us to think of words about fashion as secondary. But some ideas can only be expressed in language, and some can only be expressed in fabric. I want to do both."

"An exploration of shadow and structure. Twelve looks that move between architectural tailoring and fluid draping, rendered in a palette of midnight, charcoal, and burnt sienna."

"A long-form essay on why the most interesting clothes are the ones that show their making — raw edges, visible seams, and unfinished hems as a design philosophy."

"Structured forms in flowing fabrics. A contradiction made wearable."

"Creating is the most honest thing I know how to do."

## Quick Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Does it have at least two em dashes?
- [ ] Is there a short punchy sentence after a longer one?
- [ ] Can you touch something in the first paragraph? (fabric, scissors, a surface, a texture)
- [ ] Are there zero exclamation marks?
- [ ] Did you delete every "innovative," "iconic," "stunning," and "cutting-edge"?
- [ ] Does it end with something short and definitive?
- [ ] Would it make sense if you read it out loud at normal speed?
- [ ] Does it sound like a person wrote it at midnight in a studio, not a marketing team at 2pm?
